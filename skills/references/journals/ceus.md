## Submission Requirements

| Item | Requirement | Notes |
|------|-------------|-------|
| Total word limit | `<= 8,000 words` | Check the current author guidelines before submission |
| Abstract length | `<= 250 words` | Keep contribution, method, evidence, and result visible |
| Highlights | 3-5 items, each `<= 85` characters | Required for CEUS workflows |
| Graphical abstract | Recommended | Prepare when the paper has a strong visual narrative |
| Data availability statement | Required | Must be explicit, even when data cannot be shared |
| Review model | Double-blind | Remove author-identifying statements from the manuscript |

### Required Companion Items

- Highlights file or section with 3-5 concise contribution bullets
- Data availability statement
- Anonymous manuscript metadata for double-blind review

## Writing Preferences

### Journal Fit

- Keep a clear geography / urban-systems perspective throughout the paper.
- Show why the work matters for planning, policy, or spatial decision-making.
- Prefer concrete contribution statements over generic AI or data-science framing.

### Style Preferences

- Prefer precise, evidence-linked claims over promotional language.
- Use cautious verbs such as `suggest`, `indicate`, or `show` unless stronger causal evidence is available.
- Keep method descriptions explicit and reproducible.
- Make transition sentences functional rather than ornamental.

### Avoid

- `novel`, `groundbreaking`, `revolutionary`, or similar self-promotional adjectives
- Empty smart-city framing that is not used in the analysis
- Over-generalized policy claims unsupported by the empirical setting
- Discussion paragraphs that repeat results without interpretation

## Quality Checks

### Before Submission

- [ ] The abstract fits the CEUS length constraint.
- [ ] Highlights are 3-5 items and each stays within 85 characters.
- [ ] The manuscript keeps a visible geospatial or urban-systems angle.
- [ ] Discussion includes planning / policy relevance when appropriate.
- [ ] Claims are calibrated and not written as marketing language.
- [ ] The manuscript is anonymized for double-blind review.
- [ ] A data availability statement is present.

### Tense and Tone Checks

- Methods are usually described in the past tense.
- Results are usually reported in the past tense or present-simple summary form.
- Discussion can shift to present tense when interpreting broader implications.
- Conclusion should stay measured; avoid claiming universal validity.

## Section Guidance

### Abstract
- **Purpose:** State the research problem, approach, evidence, and principal contribution compactly.
- **Expected content:** Context, gap, method, core result, practical or theoretical implication.
- **Common pitfalls:** Too much background, missing evidence, vague contribution, no urban / spatial relevance.

### Introduction
- **Purpose:** Establish why the question matters for CEUS readers and what gap the paper addresses.
- **Expected content:** Urban / environmental systems motivation, literature gap, contribution, paper roadmap.
- **Common pitfalls:** Generic AI framing, weak journal fit, contribution stated too late.

### Study Area / Data / Methods
- **Purpose:** Show the spatial setting, data sources, and analytical workflow clearly enough for evaluation.
- **Expected content:** Study area rationale, data provenance, preprocessing, model / analytical design, evaluation choices.
- **Common pitfalls:** Missing spatial context, missing reproducibility details, under-explained study area selection.

### Results
- **Purpose:** Present empirical findings with clear comparison, scale, and evidence.
- **Expected content:** Main findings, heterogeneity or subgroup results, quantitative comparisons, figure/table references.
- **Common pitfalls:** Reporting metrics without interpretation, overclaiming, hiding negative or mixed findings.

### Discussion
- **Purpose:** Interpret what the findings mean for urban systems, planning, or policy.
- **Expected content:** Mechanism-level interpretation, comparison to prior studies, implications, limitations.
- **Common pitfalls:** Repeating results, overstating generalizability, skipping practical meaning.

### Conclusion
- **Purpose:** Close with the main takeaways, bounded contribution, and realistic next steps.
- **Expected content:** Short recap, contribution statement, implication, future work.
- **Common pitfalls:** Inflated claims, introducing new evidence, overly broad policy promises.
