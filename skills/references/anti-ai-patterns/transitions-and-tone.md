# Transition and Tone Patterns

Category focus: overly smooth transitions, polished filler, and tonal choices that make text sound generic rather than authorial.

## High Risk

| Problem expression | Replacement | Why risky |
|--------------------|-------------|-----------|
| Moreover, it is worth noting that [point]. | Moreover, [point]. | Double-padding in one sentence |
| In conclusion, it is clear that [claim]. | In conclusion, these findings suggest that [claim]. | Wraps conclusion in unnecessary certainty |
| Taken as a whole, this underscores the critical importance of [point]. | Taken together, these findings indicate that [point]. | Sounds polished but vague |

## Medium Risk

| Problem expression | Replacement | Why risky |
|--------------------|-------------|-----------|
| Furthermore, [generic reinforcement]. | Use only if the logical addition is real | Repeated transition stacking is a common AI signature |
| On the other hand, [point]. | In contrast, [point]. / omit | Often misused when there is no true opposition |
| This aligns with the broader trend that [claim]. | This pattern is consistent with prior findings on [topic]. | Generic tone without citation-ready specificity |
| From a broader perspective, [claim]. | At the city scale / policy level / neighborhood level, [claim]. | Abstract framing sounds synthetic |

## Optional

| Problem expression | Replacement | Why risky |
|--------------------|-------------|-----------|
| Overall, [claim]. | Omit or move to conclusion | Fine once, weak when repeated in many sections |
| In essence, [claim]. | [Claim]. | Stylistic filler |
| Importantly, [claim]. | [Claim]. / specify the importance | Acceptable if used sparingly and with evidence |
